A year ago I quit my job at a software company in Bangkok and moved to Chiang Mai with a vague idea and a spreadsheet of savings that would last me about eighteen months if I was careful.

I wasn't always careful.

Here's what actually happened, stripped of the narrative I'd be tempted to put on it for a Twitter thread.

## What I built

Three things, with varying results:

1. **A small SaaS tool** for freelance translators to manage Thai/English project invoicing. Still running. ~12 paying customers. Revenue covers about 40% of my expenses. Not a hit, but alive.

2. **A short course** on setting up hydroponic systems for urban Thai households. Sold once to a friend who was being kind. Shelved.

3. **Consulting work** — helping small businesses in Chiang Mai think through their software choices and workflows. This is where I've earned most of my income. I didn't plan for this. It happened because people asked.

## What I learned about myself

**I overestimate what I can build in a month and underestimate what I can learn in a year.**

The translation SaaS was supposed to take six weeks. It took five months. But by the end of those five months I understood customer acquisition, payment processing in Southeast Asia, and support work at a level I couldn't have learned from any other approach.

**I need a forcing function.**

When I have infinite time to work on something, I work on it infinitely and ship nothing. My best months were the ones where I had an external deadline — a customer waiting, a launch I'd announced publicly, a fixed consulting commitment. The structure I resented was the structure I needed.

**"Passion" is overrated. Curiosity is better.**

I'm not passionate about invoicing software. I am curious about why translators keep using spreadsheets and WhatsApp when better tools exist. Curiosity sustains better than passion when the work gets boring, which it will.

## What I got wrong

**I thought being solo was the hard part.** It's not. Being the only person who cares whether a thing ships is the hard part. The loneliness is real, but it's manageable. The accountability gap is the real problem.

**I underestimated the importance of talking to people.** I spent the first three months building before I'd properly spoken to twenty potential users. Wasted time. The conversations I had in month four changed the product more than six months of solo thinking had.

**I optimised for comfort too early.** A nice apartment, a good coffee shop, a comfortable routine. These things have their place, but I used them as a way to feel productive without being productive. The creative discomfort I was avoiding was actually where the ideas lived.

## What I'd tell myself at the start

Start talking to people before you start building.

Keep your expenses lower than you think you need to, for longer than you think you should.

The consulting work you're embarrassed to do because it feels like "not building a product" is paying your rent and teaching you things your product never will. Don't resent it.

Chiang Mai is a good place to do this work. The people here who are building things are genuinely interesting. Find them.
