Eight months ago I bought a roll of black irrigation tubing, a cheap water pump, and a bag of clay pebbles. I told myself I was going to grow my own food. I had no idea what I was doing.

I still don't, not fully. But the plants are alive and I'm learning something new every week.

## The setup

I'm running a Nutrient Film Technique (NFT) system — a method where a thin film of nutrient-rich water flows continuously over the roots in angled channels. The roots dangle in air, only their tips touching the stream. It sounds precarious, and it is.

My current configuration:

- **6 channels**, each 1.5 metres long, 110mm PVC pipe
- **40-litre reservoir** underneath, painted white to reflect heat
- **Timer-controlled pump** running 15 minutes on, 15 minutes off (learned this the hard way after root rot in week three)
- **LED grow light**, 200W draw, full spectrum, running 16 hours/day
- **pH and EC meters** — non-negotiable; I ignored pH for the first month and wondered why my basil looked sad

Total build cost: around ฿4,500 (~$125 USD). I could have spent more. I'm glad I didn't.

## What grows well here

Chiang Mai has a problem: it gets very hot from March to May. Even indoors, ambient temperatures can hit 35°C, which stresses most leafy greens. Here's what I've found:

| Crop | Result | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| Butterhead lettuce | ✓ Excellent | Bolt-resistant variety matters |
| Thai basil (โหระพา) | ✓ Excellent | Thrives in the heat |
| Spinach | ✗ Poor | Bolts immediately in hot months |
| Pak choi | ✓ Good | Fast turnover, ~4 weeks |
| Coriander | ✗ Inconsistent | Sensitive to light cycles |
| Spring onions | ✓ Very good | Low maintenance, almost foolproof |

The lesson: grow what wants to grow in your climate, not what you read about in a YouTube video filmed in Oregon.

## The failures I didn't expect

### 1. Algae

If light hits your reservoir, you get algae. Simple as that. I lost two weeks of growth in month two because green slime was competing with my plants for nutrients. Fix: double-wrap the reservoir in black plastic, tape every seam.

### 2. Power cuts

Chiang Mai has occasional power interruptions, especially during storm season. When the pump stops, the roots dry out within 30–40 minutes in hot weather. I added a small battery backup (a repurposed UPS unit from a computer shop on Nimman) that buys me about 3 hours. Enough time to notice and act.

### 3. Nutrient imbalance

I started with a generic "A+B" hydroponic nutrient solution because it was cheap and easy. My plants grew, but they looked pale and leggy. Switched to a three-part formula and calibrated EC to 1.2–1.8 mS/cm depending on growth stage. The difference was visible within a week.

## Why I'm still doing it

The honest answer: it's not about the cost savings. A head of lettuce from the market costs ฿15. My lettuce probably costs me ฿40 when I account for electricity and nutrients.

It's about something else.

There's something deeply satisfying about eating food you grew under conditions you controlled. Every harvest is feedback. You get a direct, unambiguous signal about whether your decisions were correct — something that's surprisingly rare when you work on software products.

It's also made me think differently about food systems, supply chains, and what "local" actually means. That's a longer post for another day.
